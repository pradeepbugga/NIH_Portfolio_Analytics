You are classifying the PRIMARY INTENT of an NIH grant.

Task: 
Determine whether this grant is primarily OBSERVATIONAL EPIDEMIOLOGY.

Definition of OBSERVATIONAL EPIDEMIOLOGY: 
The central goal is to analyze human populations, cohorts, or real-world data to IDENTIFY, QUANTIFY, or UNDERSTAND associations between exposures, risk factors, biomarkers, or behaviors and health outcomes — WITHOUT manipulating interventions.

Key criteria (must be central, not incidental):
- Uses population-level or cohort data (e.g., longitudinal studies, registries, EHRs, surveys)
- Estimates prevalence, incidence, risk factors, or associations
- Studies relationships between variables (e.g., exposure → outcome, biomarker → outcome)
- May include statistical modeling or machine learning, but ONLY as a tool to analyze observational data

NOT observational epidemiology if the primary goal is:
- Developing or validating a diagnostic tool or classifier for clinical use (diagnostic)
- Building general-purpose methods, assays, or platforms (research tool)
- Studying biological or molecular mechanisms (mechanistic)
- Testing an intervention, treatment, or therapy (therapeutic)
- Building predictive models intended for clinical decision-making rather than understanding associations (diagnostic/clinical)
- Developing data infrastructure, cores, or shared resources (infrastructure)

Critical distinctions (edge cases):
- If the goal is to UNDERSTAND relationships (e.g., risk factors, associations) → observational epidemiology
- If the goal is to PREDICT or CLASSIFY patients for clinical use → diagnostic (NOT observational)
- If machine learning is used to improve understanding of population patterns → observational
- If machine learning is used to build a deployable clinical prediction tool → NOT observational
- If biomarkers are studied to understand associations → observational
- If biomarkers are used to build a diagnostic test → NOT observational
